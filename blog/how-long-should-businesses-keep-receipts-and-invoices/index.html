How Long Should Businesses Keep Receipts and Invoices?

Updated June 18, 2026 by DocCraftTools Editorial Team

By DocCraftTools Editorial Team

Good record keeping is one of the most important habits for any business. Whether you are a freelancer, consultant, contractor, landlord or small business owner, maintaining receipts and invoices helps you track income, manage expenses, prepare taxes and resolve disputes when they arise.

Many business owners know they should keep records, but they are often unsure how long documents should be retained. While requirements vary by country and industry, there are several general best practices that can help businesses stay organized and prepared.

Why Receipts and Invoices Matter

Receipts and invoices serve as evidence of financial transactions. They help document income received, expenses paid, services delivered and products sold. Without proper records, businesses may struggle to verify transactions or support accounting and tax reporting.

  • Track business income
  • Verify expenses
  • Support tax filings
  • Resolve customer disputes
  • Maintain accounting records
  • Prepare for audits
  • Monitor business performance

What Is the Difference Between an Invoice and a Receipt?

An invoice is a document that requests payment for goods or services. A receipt is a document that confirms payment has already been received.

Many businesses issue both documents during the same transaction lifecycle.

  1. Quotation provided
  2. Work approved
  3. Invoice issued
  4. Payment received
  5. Receipt issued

How Long Should Businesses Keep Records?

The exact retention period depends on local laws, tax regulations and industry requirements. However, many businesses follow a retention period of at least five to seven years for important financial documents.

This includes:

  • Invoices
  • Receipts
  • Bank statements
  • Expense records
  • Tax filings
  • Payroll records
  • Business contracts

Before destroying records, businesses should always verify local legal and tax requirements.

Why Long-Term Record Retention Is Important

Tax Compliance

Tax authorities may require businesses to provide supporting documentation for previous tax returns. Proper record retention helps demonstrate that income and expenses were reported accurately.

Audit Preparation

If a business is audited, receipts and invoices provide evidence for transactions and accounting entries.

Customer Disputes

Invoices and receipts can help resolve disagreements about payments, deliverables or billing history.

Financial Analysis

Historical records help businesses understand growth trends, seasonal patterns and long-term performance.

Digital vs Paper Records

Many businesses now store documents digitally rather than relying entirely on paper copies.

Digital storage offers several advantages:

  • Reduced physical storage space
  • Faster searching and retrieval
  • Better backup options
  • Easier document sharing
  • Improved organization

Best Practices for Organizing Records

Use Consistent File Names

Create naming conventions that make documents easy to locate.

  • INV-2026-001.pdf
  • Receipt-2026-04-15.pdf
  • ClientABC-Invoice-014.pdf

Organize by Year

Create folders for each year and separate income, expenses and tax records.

Back Up Important Files

Maintain backups using secure cloud storage or external drives.

Keep Documents Readable

Ensure digital copies remain accessible and are not stored in outdated formats that may become difficult to open in the future.

Common Record Keeping Mistakes

  • Throwing documents away too early
  • Keeping no backup copies
  • Using inconsistent naming systems
  • Mixing personal and business records

Recommended Retention Strategy

A practical approach for many small businesses is:

  • Store invoices digitally
  • Store receipts digitally
  • Keep records organized by year
  • Maintain multiple backups
  • Retain records for at least seven years unless local regulations require otherwise

Final Thoughts

Receipts and invoices are more than simple transaction documents. They support accounting, tax reporting, business planning and customer communication. Keeping records organized and retaining them for an appropriate period helps businesses reduce risk and operate more efficiently.
